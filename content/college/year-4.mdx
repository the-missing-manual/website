Placement season is a grind. Depending on your college and field, it runs for several months. Some students get placed early. Others are interviewing for six or seven months.

What you have going in matters more than what you do in the final two weeks before your first interview.

### Algorithmic Preparation

Work through problems at the difficulty level your target companies test. The distribution matters more than the volume. Understand why each solution works. If someone changes the constraints, you should be able to adapt. Memorized solutions don't adapt.

Medium-difficulty algorithmic problems are the core of most industry interviews. Harder problems come up at the most selective companies. Know which category you're targeting and focus your preparation there.

After solving a problem, explain it out loud as if explaining it to someone. If you can't explain it, you haven't understood it.

### Resume

One page. Honest. Specific.

Projects with live links. Numbers where you have them. If your project had users, say how many. If it reduced something, say by how much. Vague project descriptions read as vague.

Write your resume as if the person reading it doesn't have context about your college or city. They need the facts on the page.

### Communication

You will talk to a lot of strangers about your code. Practice explaining your projects out loud. Not a rehearsed script. A clear explanation of what you built, why you built it, and what was technically hard about it.

The ability to explain a technical decision clearly separates candidates who can code from candidates who can work in a team.

### Aptitude Rounds

Some companies run an aptitude filter before any technical round. The questions are typically quantitative reasoning, logical reasoning, and verbal ability. Students with stronger technical skills than their peers get eliminated at this stage every year because they didn't prepare for it.

Prepare for it. Search for aptitude practice resources. Work through problems daily in the months before placement season starts.

### The Round Structure

Most hiring processes follow a pattern. The exact number and order of rounds varies by company.

| Round | What It Tests |
|-------|--------------|
| Aptitude | Quantitative and logical reasoning |
| Coding test | Algorithmic problems under time pressure |
| Technical round | DSA, your projects, core CS concepts |
| Technical round (deeper) | System design or more depth on prior round |
| HR round | Communication, fit, expectations |

Prepare for all of them. Not equally, but don't leave any completely unaddressed.

### CS Fundamentals

Technical interviews at most companies beyond the service tier test foundational CS subjects.

**Operating Systems:** Processes, threads, scheduling, memory management, virtual memory, deadlocks.

**Database Management Systems:** SQL (joins, subqueries, indexing, transactions), normalization, query optimization basics. Be able to write non-trivial queries without looking anything up.

**Computer Networks:** The network stack, HTTP, DNS, TLS basics, the difference between TCP and UDP. Know what happens when you type a URL in a browser. That question comes up often.

**Object-Oriented Programming:** The core principles and how they relate to design decisions. Design patterns come up occasionally.

You don't need expert depth before your first interview. You need enough to have a real conversation when the topic comes up.

### Common Questions

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="How long does placement season actually last?">
    Several months, not a few weeks. Some students are placed early; others interview for six or seven months. Plan your energy for a season, not a sprint — see [Wellbeing](/wellbeing).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Should I study the night before an interview?">
    Review what you already know and stop early. A concept you haven't fixed by the night before will not get fixed in the last two hours, and lost sleep costs you more than the extra practice gains. See [Wellbeing](/wellbeing).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="I keep getting eliminated in the aptitude round. Why?">
    Aptitude is a separate skill from coding, and strong engineers get filtered here every year because they skipped it. Practice quantitative, logical, and verbal reasoning daily in the months before the season.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if I don't get placed on campus?">
    More common and less final than it feels. Off-campus applications, startups, and freelance work all stay open. See [After College](/college/after-college).
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
