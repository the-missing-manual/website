You don't know where to start. You've read threads, watched YouTube, maybe bought a course, and you're more confused than when you began. Everyone has an opinion and most of them contradict each other.

That's not a you problem. The information landscape around "how to learn programming" is genuinely bad. It's full of content made for engagement, not for people who need to go from zero to something real.

Here's a clearer frame.

### The Mindset Before the Tools

Every piece of software that exists was built because someone found a gap. Discord, Google, Swiggy, the notes app on your phone. Someone used something, got annoyed by what it couldn't do, and built a version that fixed that.

That's the engineer mindset: <Mark>if something costs you five minutes every time you do it, that five-minute problem is worth forty hours of your time to solve.</Mark> You're not saving five minutes once. You're saving it every time, forever.

You don't have the skills yet to solve most problems. That's fine. What you need to do before your program starts is build the habit of noticing gaps. Write them down. Even if you can't build anything yet, practice the instinct.

When you finally pick up your first language, you'll have a list of things you actually want to make. That list is worth more than any roadmap.

### Don't Pick a Language. Pick a Project.

The standard advice is "start with X language." This is the wrong starting question.

Some problems are better solved in certain languages. But for a beginner, the real reason to pick a language is simpler: pick the one that will help you finish the project you already want to build.

Start with what you want to build. Then research what people use to build that type of thing. The [choosing a language](/programming/language-choice) page covers how to think through this without someone making the decision for you.

<Callout type="info">
The goal is to finish something. Not to pick the "right" language. Finishing a small, ugly project teaches you more than reading three books about which language to start with.
</Callout>

### Finding Your First Project Idea

If nothing comes to you immediately, start here: what do you do every day that's annoying?

You copy something from one place to another manually. You track something in a notes app that could be automated. You search for something repeatedly that could be a tool. You message yourself files because sharing between devices is irritating.

Some examples of small first projects that are achievable within weeks of starting:

- A script that renames files in a folder by a pattern
- A bot that does one thing (reminds you of something, posts a daily message)
- A small webpage that shows something you care about (your schedule, a tracker)
- A small tool that tells you the weather for your city

None of these are impressive. All of them are real. When you finish one, you'll know what you didn't know before. That knowledge points you to the next thing to build.

<Callout type="warn">
Don't start with an app idea that requires user accounts, payments, and a mobile interface. That's not a beginner project. That's a company. Start smaller by several orders of magnitude.
</Callout>

### What to Actually Do, Practically

Before your program starts, the goal is not to become a programmer. It's to become someone who has finished at least one thing and isn't starting from scratch on day one.

Here's a concrete sequence:

**Week 1 to 2:** Pick a language based on your project idea. Spend two weeks on the basics only: variables, loops, conditions, functions. Don't try to be thorough. Try to be fast.

**Week 3 to 4:** Start the project. It will break immediately. Look up why. Fix it. This cycle of breaking and fixing is the actual learning. The tutorials are scaffolding. The project is the building.

**Week 5 onward:** Finish something, no matter how ugly. Then ask: what would make this better? That question generates your next project.

Search for introductory resources in the language you chose. Look for ones that are project-based, not just lecture-heavy. Free options exist for almost every language. Verify that a resource is recent and well-reviewed before investing significant time in it.

<Callout type="info">
Avoid tutorial hell. It's the loop where you watch tutorial after tutorial and never build anything yourself. If you've been following a tutorial for more than two weeks without building something yourself, stop and build something yourself.
</Callout>

### Communities Help More Than Courses

You will get stuck. When you do, you need a place to ask.

Asking people in a community is faster and more specific than searching the same question for three hours. You'll also start to see what problems other beginners have, which teaches you things no course covers.

Search for communities around whatever you're building: programming Discord servers by language and topic, subreddits for beginners, forums for the framework or tool you're using. Most popular tools have active communities.

Before asking a question, read the [how to ask for help](/resources/how-to-ask) page. A well-framed question gets a useful answer. A vague one gets ignored or a question back.

<Mark>The people you learn alongside matter. Find at least one person at roughly your level who you can share progress with.</Mark> It sounds small. It isn't.

### What Your Program Will Expect

You don't need to be good at programming before your program starts. You need to not be starting from scratch in the first semester when everyone else has some base.

Most technical programs teach a foundational language in the first year. If you've already written code in any language, that won't be entirely new territory. The logic transfers. The syntax is learnable in days.

If you're planning a computing-related program and haven't opened a code editor yet, open one now. Even if all you do is write a "hello world" and understand what it did, that's a step forward.
