title: Second Year

Second year is when students split. Some go into building software. Some go into algorithmic problem-solving. A few do both seriously, but they're usually working harder than everyone else, not smarter.

<Mark>The students who try to do both seriously in second year often burn out in third year. Decide which one is primary.</Mark>

### Pick a Direction

The question to answer by the start of second year: do you want to build software, or do you want to be strong at algorithmic problem-solving for placement tests? Most companies want some of both. But where you spend your time in second year shapes which one you're actually good at.

This is not a permanent decision. It is a resource allocation decision for the next twelve months.

**Building direction.** Pick a domain and go deep. Build something real. Not a practice project. Something that does something you could explain to a non-technical person and have them understand why it exists. Deploy it somewhere. Put it in front of actual users, even if those users are just people in your hostel.

Goals for the building direction this year:

- A complete project with a working backend, a frontend, and a real database
- At least one deployment you can share a link to
- One open source contribution, however small

**Algorithmic direction.** Start structured practice here. Work through a comprehensive topic list: arrays, strings, hashing, two-pointer techniques, sliding window, linked lists, stacks, queues, binary search, trees, graphs, dynamic programming. Roughly in that order.

Don't try to do two hundred problems fast. Do twenty problems per topic slowly enough that you understand the patterns, not just the solutions. After solving a problem, explain it out loud. If you can't explain it, you haven't understood it.

### CGPA

Keep your grade average above any thresholds your target companies use. Some companies filter applicants at a cutoff before anyone reads the resume. Know what those cutoffs are and stay above them.

### Events and Communities

Second year is a good time to start attending hackathons. Not to win them yet, but to learn what you don't know and meet people who are further along. Most of what you learn in a hackathon happens in the final hours when things break and you have to fix them fast.

Search for tech fests, online hackathons, and student developer events in your area. Communities built around specific domains (open source, competitive programming, design) often run regular events. Find one in your area of focus.
