Every college server runs the same argument on loop: does CGPA matter or not. Both camps are half right, which is why it never resolves. The honest answer depends on what you're aiming at, and it's more specific than either side admits.

<Mark>CGPA works like a filter, not a score. Past the cutoff, a higher number rarely wins you anything. Below it, nothing else about you gets read.</Mark>

### It's a threshold, not a ranking

Many companies set a minimum: a Cumulative Grade Point Average (CGPA) of 6.0, or 6.5, or 7.0, and often no active backlogs. If you clear it, you're in the pool and the rest of your application, projects, and how you interview, decides the outcome. If you don't clear it, the resume is filtered before a human reads it.

This is what both camps miss. The gap between a 7.2 and an 8.5 almost never decides a product-company offer, so grinding for the higher number at the cost of skills is a bad trade. But a 5.8 that drops you below cutoffs closes doors before you can show anything, so treating grades as irrelevant is a bad trade too.

### Know your target's cutoff, then clear it comfortably

Find out where the companies you want set their bar. Ask seniors who sat those drives, not general internet advice. See [Reading the Room](/small-things/reading-the-room).

Then keep a comfortable margin above it. Sitting exactly on a 6.0 cutoff means one bad semester locks you out. A buffer protects you from that without demanding a 9-plus you don't need.

### Where CGPA matters more than the threshold

A high CGPA earns real weight in specific places:

- **Higher studies.** Master's admissions, both in India and abroad, weigh academics heavily. If an MS or MTech is on the table, grades are an investment, not a filter. See [After College](/college/after-college).
- **Some core and research roles.** Certain core engineering and research-oriented companies read grades as a signal of discipline.
- **The very top of campus drives.** A few companies shortlist partly on academics when they have more applicants than slots.

### The backlog problem is separate, and worse

An active backlog, a subject you haven't cleared, disqualifies you from many campus drives outright, regardless of your CGPA. Companies use "no active backlogs" as a hard filter.

If you fall behind, clearing backlogs takes priority over most other prep, because they gate access to the opportunities the prep is for. A bad semester is recoverable. See [Third Year](/college/year-3) and [Fourth Year](/college/year-4) for managing the timeline around it.

<Callout type="warn">
The two ways students get this wrong are mirror images. One sacrifices every skill for a 9.5 that no product company asked for. The other ignores grades entirely and gets filtered out before the interview they were sure they'd ace. Clear the threshold with margin, then spend the rest of your energy on skills and projects. See [Placements & ROI](/choice/placements).
</Callout>
