---
title: Third Year
description: Internships, real projects, and the gap between students becoming visible.
---

Third year is when the gap between students becomes visible. Some have a portfolio with real projects, have done a hackathon or two, have consistent algorithmic practice. Others are starting from near zero because they thought they had more time.

If you're starting from near zero in third year, you're not done. But the timeline is compressed and you need to move faster.

### Internships

The goal in third year is to get a substantive internship, either through your college's placement process or off-campus. Off-campus internships are often more available and more real than campus placements.

Places to look: professional networking platforms, company career pages filtered by intern roles, direct cold emails to founders at early-stage startups.

A working portfolio with deployed projects gets more traction than a good grade average with nothing to show. A strong grade average combined with a portfolio is the best position to be in.

When you cold email a startup founder, be specific. What you've built, what you want to work on, why that company. One short paragraph. Most people write three paragraphs about themselves. Write one paragraph about why you want to work on their specific problem.

### Building Something Serious

This is the year to push a project past "finished small thing" into something with users, a real problem being solved, and numbers behind it.

The best project ideas come from your own annoyances. What do you do every day that's slower than it should be? What do you wish existed?

When your project has real users, you'll run into real problems. Why is it slow? What happens when two users do the same thing at once? How do you store user data safely? These are the problems that teach you the most.

### Hackathons

Pick two or three hackathons this year and build something complete at each one. The projects you ship at hackathons go on your resume immediately. The problems you hit at 3 AM during a hackathon teach you things you won't find in any course.

Search for hackathons in your domain, both online and in-person. Many organizations run student-specific competitions. Look for application deadlines regularly since they move fast.

### Visibility

Set up a professional profile on LinkedIn. Not to ask for opportunities immediately, but to be findable. Connect with seniors at companies you want to work at. Not to immediately ask for referrals. To be present in their network and to learn what those roles actually look like from the inside.

A referral from someone at a company doesn't guarantee anything. But it almost always guarantees your resume gets seen instead of sitting unread in a system filter.
