The assumption most students have is that after college, things become clearer. Either you got placed or you didn't. Either you have a job or you're looking for one.

It's more complicated than that.

### If You Got Placed

A placement offer is not the finish line. The offer is the start of something different.

Most service company offers have a joining date gap of three to six months after you receive the letter. What you do in that gap shapes whether you walk in as a fresh graduate or as someone who hit the ground running.

**Use the gap.** Practice the technology stack the company uses. Read engineering blogs from companies whose codebases are public. Build something. The students who use those three to six months tend to perform better in training, which affects their initial team placement.

**Understand the role before you join.** A "software engineer" offer at a service company often means you won't write code for the first six months. You'll be in training. Some companies do a tech stack allocation round where you can influence what you end up doing. Go in knowing what you want and ask clearly.

If you have a product company offer, the onboarding is usually faster and more technical from day one. Set up your development environment before you start. Read whatever the company publishes publicly about their engineering culture. You'll absorb the specifics on the job, but the less basic setup you're doing in week one, the more you can focus on understanding the codebase.

<Callout type="info">
After your first job, your college matters less. What matters is what you built at the job, what you learned, and whether you can talk about it clearly. The transition from "fresher" to "experienced" is real and it happens faster than most students expect.
</Callout>

### If You Didn't Get Placed on Campus

This is more common than people admit and less catastrophic than it feels.

Most campus placement processes are deeply arbitrary. Companies cancel visits, change eligibility criteria, or cut hiring budgets weeks before they were supposed to show up. CGPA cutoffs filter out students who are more technically capable than the ones who passed. Getting through or not getting through campus placement tells you less about your ability than it tells you about timing and eligibility rules.

What to do:

**Off-campus applications:** LinkedIn, Naukri, AngelList (now Wellfound), direct company career pages. Apply in volume, but apply specifically. A tailored email explaining why you want that role at that company gets more attention than a mass-apply through a portal.

**Startups:** Early-stage startups hire off-campus constantly. A working GitHub with real projects and a genuine email to a founder gets more traction than you'd expect. Most large company HR pipelines are automated and impersonal. A founder at a 10-person startup read your email personally.

**Contract and freelance work:** Toptal, Upwork, Contra for remote freelance. Harder to get started than it sounds but not impossible with a portfolio. Even one small contract project gives you something to put in the "experience" section that breaks the fresher cycle.

**GATE:** If you want a government sector job, GATE opens that route. If you want a postgraduate degree in India, GATE is the entry point for many programs. It requires structured preparation, but free resources covering the full syllabus are available. Search for them.

<Callout type="warn">
Don't spend six months applying and waiting. Apply, follow up, and while that's happening, build something. A project you completed during your gap is a better answer to "what have you been doing" than six months of applications.
</Callout>

### Switching After Your First Job

Most people switch jobs after one to two years. That's normal and often necessary, especially if you started at a service company and want to move to a product role.

The switch from service to product is real and it takes work. Typical requirements for a product company hire with one to two years of experience:

- Stronger DSA than a fresher. Medium LeetCode problems you can solve in 20 to 30 minutes. Some hard ones.
- System design basics. Not expert-level, but you should be able to talk about how to design a URL shortener or a rate limiter with some coherence.
- Concrete projects or work experience. "I worked on a feature that reduced API response time by 40 percent" is worth more than "I did backend work at my previous company."

The transition is worth doing if your goal is to work on software that reaches real users and do more than maintain legacy systems. But it requires preparation. Set a six-month window, start LeetCode again, and apply once you've done the prep.

### Higher Studies

Two paths worth knowing clearly.

**MS abroad:** Target schools, SOP, GRE (sometimes optional now), LORs, and funding. The difference between a funded MS and an unfunded one is often two hundred thousand rupees in debt versus a stipend. Research funding before applying to any program. Funded programs at good schools are worth waiting for. Unfunded MS degrees at lower-ranked schools often aren't.

**MTech in India:** IIT MTech through GATE. Fully funded, gives you access to IIT research environments and placement. If you couldn't get into an IIT for undergrad and want that environment, GATE MTech is the path. The competition is real but more achievable than JEE for most people.

<Callout type="info">
A PhD is a different track entirely. If research is what you want, go for it. If you're considering a PhD because you're not sure what else to do, that's not a good reason and you'll figure that out expensively. Spend three months working in a research lab first if you can.
</Callout>
