---
title: First Year
description: Building a foundation before direction sets in.
---

First year is not the time to specialize. It is the time to build a base that everything else runs on later.

<Mark>Most programs have the lightest workload in the first year. The habits you build now are easier to build now than at any other point.</Mark>

### What to Focus On

**Learn your program's language of instruction.** Whatever language your course starts with, learn it properly. Not to pass exams, but to understand what the computer is actually doing. Memory, loops, conditions, how functions work, how data is stored. This knowledge doesn't expire. It explains things you'll read for years.

**Write code outside class.** One small project a month. A script, a tool, a bot, anything that runs and does something, even if it's trivial. The habit of finishing things matters more than the quality of what you finish in first year.

**Start using version control.** Learn how to track your work. This is not optional in any professional setting and the sooner it becomes automatic the better. Search for beginner guides on version control and source control systems.

**Protect your academic standing.** A solid grade average in first year opens doors in later years that are hard to open otherwise. Some companies set cutoffs that filter applicants before a human reads the resume. Don't obsess over a perfect score. But don't let first year slip either.

### Resume

Start a resume now, even if it feels empty. A blank resume in first year is useful. It shows you exactly what you need to build. A blank resume in final year is a problem.

Add things as you do them. A project you shipped, a course you completed, a tool you learned. The act of writing it down forces you to notice what you haven't done yet. That gap is information. It tells you what to work on next.

By the time placement season arrives, you want a resume that accumulated over three years, not one you wrote in a weekend.

### What Not to Do Yet

Don't start competitive programming seriously before you have a programming foundation. Without the base, it will feel impossible in a way that's discouraging. Give yourself the foundation first.

Don't try to learn five languages, five frameworks, and five tools simultaneously. Finish one thing and understand it before starting the next.

Don't skip the boring fundamentals. The students who skip them spend years patching gaps.

### Building the Habit

The students who come out of their program with something to show didn't wake up in final year and start. They wrote code consistently across all years, even when what they wrote was small and ugly.

First year is when that habit starts. Or doesn't.
