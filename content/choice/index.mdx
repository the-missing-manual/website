CSE is an engineering degree. Engineering means you build things.

You don't read about how a bridge works and call yourself a civil engineer. You calculate loads, pick materials, draw the structure. CS is identical. You don't read about sorting algorithms. You write them, run them, break them, and figure out why they broke.

Most Indian colleges treat CS as an academic subject. Theory-heavy exams, rote learning, answers written on paper. You pass by memorizing, not by building. <Mark>This is a structural problem with how the degree gets delivered, not with CS itself.</Mark>

AI is making software development more productive. It is not removing the need for strong engineers. The bar for what "good" means is rising. Understanding systems, debugging, architecture, and problem-solving matters more now, not less.

The students who come out of college knowing how to write software are the ones who treated college as a base and built everything else themselves. College gave them time and infrastructure. The rest they did on their own.

<Mark>The practitioner mindset, treating yourself as someone who builds and not someone who only studies, matters more than which college you pick.</Mark> A good college makes this easier. A bad one makes it harder. Neither replaces the work you put in yourself.



### The Obvious Part First

If you get into a Tier 1 college, an IIT, NIT, or IIIT, go. The environment, the peer group, the brand. It compounds over time.

Where it gets complicated is private universities. Some private CSE programs are genuinely good. Others are not. Nobody hands you a clean ranking. You have to find it yourself. Reddit threads, Discord servers with current students, LinkedIn alumni. These are worth your time before you sign anything.

### What You Are Trying to Avoid

You are not looking for a perfect college. You are trying to avoid a **bad environment**, a place where the default is to do nothing beyond assignments, where nobody runs events, where going off-syllabus is treated as a problem.

<Callout type="warn">
That matters more than where a college sits in a rankings table.
</Callout>
