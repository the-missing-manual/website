A college sometimes has decent placements purely because of brand value. That does not mean the developer culture is strong. These are different things, and conflating them is one of the more common mistakes students make when picking a college.

<Mark>The single most predictive question: how many students are actively building software outside class.</Mark> That one number predicts the quality of your four years better than any ranking, any infrastructure tour, and any placement brochure.



### What to Look For

### Look Past the Brochure

Some colleges list clubs that have not run an event in years. Check Instagram pages, GitHub orgs, and event history, not the "Clubs and Activities" section of the college website.

Look at the **practical-to-theory ratio**. Some colleges run heavy on exams and textbook content. Others have labs, live projects, and hackathons built into the schedule. Ask students which describes theirs.



### Curriculum Matters Less Than You Think

Most students spend time comparing syllabi. AI elective, Blockchain module, GenAI specialization. This mostly does not matter.

The strongest developers learn those things on their own. What holds up over time are the fundamentals:

- **Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA)**
- **Operating Systems (OS)**
- **Computer Networks**
- **Databases**
- **System Design**

<Callout type="info">
A flashy syllabus is not a better syllabus. If the college teaches DSA well and gives you time and resources to build things, you are in a better position than someone at a college with a "Generative AI" elective and no working lab culture.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="If your goal is research">
If you are aiming for an MS or PhD rather than industry, look at faculty publications, undergraduate research opportunities, and active research labs. Placement statistics become largely irrelevant. The college's research output is what matters.
</Callout>
