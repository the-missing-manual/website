title: Reaching Out
description: Email that gets read, cold messages that get replies, and treating English as a skill you practice.

A large part of your career runs on messages to people you don't know well: a senior for advice, a professor for a reference, a stranger for a referral. Most students send these badly, get no reply, and conclude that networking doesn't work for them. The message was the problem, not the network.

None of this is about being fake. It is about respecting the other person's time enough that they want to spend some of it on you.

<Mark>The person you're messaging is busy and owes you nothing. A message that respects that gets a reply. One that doesn't gets ignored, and you never find out why.</Mark>

### The message that gets ignored

The classic dead end is a single line: "Hello sir." Then nothing. The other person now has to ask what you want, so they don't, and the conversation ends before it starts.

Send the greeting and the actual question together, in one message. Say who you are in a line, what you want, and why you're asking this specific person. Make it answerable without a back-and-forth.

### Email like a busy person is reading it

A professor or recruiter reads a lot of email. Yours competes with all of it.

- **Write a real subject line.** "Question about the DBMS project deadline" beats a blank subject or "hi".
- **Say your name and context up front.** They don't have your roll number memorized.
- **Make one clear ask.** Five questions in one email get zero answered.
- **Write in full sentences.** SMS-speak in a professional email reads as carelessness, and it colors how they read the rest.

### Cold outreach that gets a reply

Messaging a stranger for a referral or advice works, but the mass-forwarded "please refer me" gets marked as spam and ignored. What separates a reply from silence:

- **Show you picked them on purpose.** One specific line about their work, their post, or their team beats any template.
- **Keep it short.** A few lines. A wall of text asks for more attention than a stranger will give.
- **Don't attach your resume in the first message.** It reads as a mass send. Offer it once they reply.
- **Follow up once,** a few days later, gently. Then stop. One nudge is fine. A second is pestering.

### English is a skill you practice, not a trait you have

Many students treat their English as fixed, decide it's weak, and avoid speaking. Interviews then punish the avoidance, not the accent, because fumbling for words in a technical round costs you selections you'd otherwise clear.

It gets better the same way code does, with reps. Speak in class. Give the presentation you'd rather skip. Join a discussion. Explain a concept to a batchmate out loud. The point is not a perfect accent. It is being able to say what you mean without freezing, and that only comes from doing it.

<Callout type="info">
In a live round, take a beat before you answer. A short pause to gather the thought reads as composure. Rushing into a jumbled sentence reads as panic. The [Placement Season](/wellbeing/placement-season) page covers thinking out loud when you're stuck.
</Callout>

### Small habits that keep the door open

- **Send the two-line thank-you.** After anyone helps you, a short thanks costs nothing and makes them far likelier to help again. Almost nobody sends it, which is exactly why it stands out.
- **Lurk before you post.** Every community, an open-source project, a Discord, a forum, has norms. Read a week of it before your first post so you don't faceplant on the way in.
- **Ask one question after a talk or meetup.** The speaker remembers the person who asked. It is the cheapest visibility there is.
