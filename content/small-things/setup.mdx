Your setup is the boring layer under everything else you do. Nobody grades it, so nobody teaches it, and most students only think about it the day it fails. That day is usually the worst possible one: the morning of an online assessment, or the week a final-year project is due.

A few one-time decisions keep that day from arriving.

<Mark>The cost of setting this up is an afternoon. The cost of skipping it is a dead drive the week your project is due, or a locked account the morning of an interview.</Mark>

### Back up your work before you lose it

Every semester, someone's final-year project dies on a drive that stopped spinning, and there is no second copy. The fix costs nothing while the work still exists.

- **Keep two copies in two places.** Your laptop plus a cloud folder, or your laptop plus an external drive. One copy is not a backup.
- **Put code in version control.** A repository is a backup with a history. You get every past version, and the copy lives on a server, not only your machine. Push often.
- **Automate it or you won't do it.** A folder that syncs to the cloud on its own beats a manual copy you keep meaning to make.

<Callout type="info">
Version control is worth learning early even if the only project is yours alone. It backs up your work, records how it changed over time, and is the same tool every job expects you to use. Starting on a small solo project is far easier than learning it under pressure later.
</Callout>

### Name and file things so future-you can find them

The `final_v2_ACTUAL_new(1).pdf` on your desktop is a habit, and it has a cost. The cost arrives when a form asks for your marksheet in the next ten minutes and you can't find which of six files it is.

- **Pick one naming pattern and hold it.** A date and a short label, like `2026-07-resume.pdf`, sorts itself and never collides.
- **Keep one folder for documents that matter.** Marksheets, ID proofs, your resume, certificates. When an application asks for them, you go to one place.
- **Do a five-minute cleanup on a schedule.** Once a week beats once a year, when the mess is too big to face.

### One leaked password shouldn't cost you everything

By your second year you have accounts in the dozens: college portal, GitHub, LinkedIn, email, coding platforms, cloud services. Studies put a typical university student near 70 to 100 accounts. Reusing one password across them means a single leak opens all of them.

- **Use a password manager.** It generates and remembers a different password for every account, so you memorize one and it handles the rest. Several are free.
- **Turn on two-factor authentication** for email, GitHub, and anything tied to money or identity. Your email is the reset path for everything else, so protect it first.
- **Keep recovery codes somewhere real,** not only on the phone you might lose.

### Your student email is worth actual money

The email your college issued unlocks a pile of professional tools for free, and most students never claim any of it.

- **The GitHub Student Developer Pack** bundles free access to developer tools, cloud credits, domains, and courses. Search for it, verify with your college email, and browse what's inside.
- **Cloud providers run student tiers** with credits you can use to host your first project instead of paying or never deploying it.
- **Student discounts exist on most software and hardware.** Check before you pay full price for anything.

<Callout type="warn">
Claim these while you can, because they die with the college email. The moment you graduate, that address usually stops working, and any account you registered on it, along with unclaimed benefits, goes with it. Register anything you want to keep, like GitHub, LinkedIn, and certificate accounts, on a personal email you control for life. Use the college email only to verify student status.
</Callout>

### Assume the exam-day network will fail

Online assessments are timed, and the timer does not pause because your Wi-Fi dropped or the power went out. A disconnection mid-test can end the attempt with no retry. This is a known, avoidable failure.

- **Have a backup way online.** A phone hotspot with data left on it, tested before the day, not during it.
- **Have backup power.** A charged laptop, a charged phone, and knowing your building's outage pattern.
- **Have a quiet, stable spot** picked in advance. Scrambling for one after the test starts is time off your clock.

The point is not paranoia. It is that these tests give you no second chance, so the cheap insurance is worth it.
