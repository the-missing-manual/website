---
title: Keeping a Record
description: A brag document, a learned-log, READMEs, and screenshots. Small records that write your resume for you.
---

Your memory is worse than you think, and it fails at the worst time: the night you sit down to write a resume, or the morning of an interview when you need a concrete story. Everything you did is in there somewhere, blurry and out of reach. The fix is to write things down as they happen, when the detail is still sharp.

These are all small, ongoing records. Each one costs a minute in the moment and saves you an evening later.

<Mark>You will not remember the detail that makes a good interview answer. You'll remember that something happened, not the number, the tradeoff, or the fix. Write it down when it's fresh, or lose it.</Mark>

### Write down wins when they happen

A brag document is a running list of what you did and what came of it. Standard practice in industry, unheard of in college. You keep one line per thing: what you built or fixed, and the result.

When resume season or an appraisal arrives, you're not staring at a blank page trying to recall a year. You're editing a list you already have. The specific detail, the load time you cut, the bug you traced, the feature you shipped, is right there, because you wrote it the day it happened.

### Keep a one-line learned-log

Every day you fix something or learn something, write a single line about it. People call this a "today I learned" log or a developer journal.

- It turns into resume bullets and interview stories, for free, over time.
- It shows you your own progress, which is worth a lot in the stretches where you feel stuck.
- One line is enough. The habit dies the moment it feels like homework.

### Record the thing while it still works

Demos die. Links rot. A hackathon deployment gets taken down a week later, and the project you're proudest of now shows a blank page to the recruiter who clicks it.

Before that happens, capture it. A short screen recording of the project working, a few screenshots of the interface. Those don't expire. When someone asks to see your work and the live link is dead, you still have proof it existed and ran.

### Put a README on every project

A recruiter or interviewer who opens your repository gives it about thirty seconds. With no README, they see a pile of files and move on. Ten minutes of writing changes what those thirty seconds tell them.

A short README says what the project does, why you built it, and how to run it. That's it. It is the difference between a repository that looks abandoned and one that looks like you cared, and it costs almost nothing.

### Save the praise

When a professor, a senior, or a user says your work was good, screenshot it. It feels awkward, and it's worth it.

An interview asks for evidence, not adjectives. "A senior said my tool saved the team time" lands harder with the message to back it. Kind words evaporate from memory in weeks. A screenshot doesn't.

### Commit small, commit often, with real messages

Version control keeps a history of your work. How you use it becomes a record too.

- **Small, focused commits** with messages that say what changed read as care. "Fixed login validation" beats "stuff" and beats one giant commit named "final".
- **It costs nothing as a student,** and anyone who opens your history, a reviewer, an interviewer, a future teammate, reads it as professionalism you already have.
