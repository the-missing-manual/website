You are about to spend a large share of your waking life at a keyboard. The body you do it with is the one you keep. Almost nobody thinks about this until something starts to hurt, and by then the fix is harder than the prevention would have been.

None of this is fitness advice. It is about staying comfortable enough to think for long stretches, which is the actual job.

<Mark>The keyboard and the chair are the tools you touch most and think about least. A few small adjustments now save you real pain in third year, when you can least afford it.</Mark>

### Learn to touch type

Most students hunt and peck for years and never question it. Then a timed assessment lands, the clock is running, and half their attention goes to finding keys instead of solving the problem.

Touch typing is typing without looking at your hands. It is worth a few weeks because:

- **It frees your attention.** When your fingers know where the keys are, all of your focus goes to the problem, not the search.
- **It matters under time pressure.** Online assessments and live coding are timed. Fumbling the keyboard is a tax on every one of those minutes.
- **It is a one-time cost.** A few weeks of deliberate practice, and the skill is yours for good.

Free typing trainers exist. Search for one, practice a little each day, and stop looking at your hands. The goal is not a speed record. It is not looking down.

### Your wrists and back will send you a bill

Coding hunched over a laptop on a hostel bed feels fine at nineteen. Roughly one in four people in keyboard-heavy work develop some repetitive strain injury: wrist pain, tingling fingers, a sore neck or back. The early signs are easy to ignore, which is exactly why people ignore them until they can't.

The fixes are cheap and mostly one-time:

- **Get your screen to eye level.** A laptop on a desk forces you to look down, which bends your neck for hours. A stand, or a stack of books, brings the screen up.
- **Keep your wrists straight, not bent.** A cheap external keyboard and mouse let you raise the screen and still type at a natural angle. On a laptop alone you can't do both.
- **Sit so your feet are flat and your forearms are parallel to the floor.** Elbows near your body, not reaching forward.
- **Break the stillness.** Stand up, walk, and shake out your hands every so often. The body is not built to hold one position for hours.

<Callout type="warn">
Take the early signs seriously. Tingling fingers, a wrist that aches after a long session, or a neck that won't loosen are warnings, not background noise. Prevention is a stand and an external keyboard. Treatment, once it becomes chronic, can mean months of not coding at full capacity, and placement season is the worst time to find that out.
</Callout>

### Give your eyes and sleep a chance

**Eyes.** Hours of staring at a bright screen in a dark room strains your eyes and gives you headaches. Every so often, look at something far away for a moment. Turn the brightness down to match the room instead of blazing in the dark.

**Sleep.** The 4 AM coding culture in hostels gets treated as a badge of honor. It mostly buys you slower thinking the next day. Working memory degrades measurably when you're short on sleep, so a problem that takes twenty minutes rested takes far longer tired. This is physiology, not motivation.
