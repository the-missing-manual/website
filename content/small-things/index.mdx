None of this is on a syllabus. No lecture covers how to name your files, how to message a senior, or why your wrists ache by third year. The advice you hear jumps straight to algorithms and projects and assumes the rest sorts itself out.

It doesn't sort itself out. The students who look a year ahead of everyone else mostly got there through a stack of small habits, each one dull on its own. This section collects the ones that pay off and stay cheap to start.

<Mark>One small habit rarely matters. Twenty of them, held for three years, are the difference you keep mistaking for talent.</Mark>

None of this is required. Pick what fits your situation and skip the rest, the same way you'd use the rest of this guide. A habit you actually keep beats five you read about and drop.

### Common Questions

<Accordions>
  <Accordion title="Do I actually need any of this to get placed?">
    No single item here decides anything. That is the point. These are cheap habits that stack, and the stack is what shows up two years later as work that looks put-together. Treat the section as a menu, not a checklist.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Isn't touch typing a waste of time?">
    You'll spend the next few decades at a keyboard. A few weeks of practice so you stop looking at your hands frees your attention for the actual problem, which matters most under a timed assessment. See [Typing and Your Body](/small-things/typing-and-body).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="I'm already in final year. Is it too late?">
    Some of this pays off over years and some pays off this week. Back up your work, claim your student pack before it expires, keep a record for interviews, and reach out without fumbling the first message. None of those need a head start. See [Your Setup](/small-things/setup) and [Keeping a Record](/small-things/keeping-a-record).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Some of this sounds like productivity theater.">
    Fair. The line is simple: if a habit produces something you can point to later, a backup, a record, a working demo, a reply from a senior, keep it. If it only produces the feeling of being organized, drop it.
  </Accordion>
</Accordions>
