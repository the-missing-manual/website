title: Reading the Room
description: Watching before you speak, disagreeing in private, and why seniors are the best source on campus.
---

Every place you walk into, a college club, an internship, your first team, runs on rules nobody wrote down. How people talk to each other, when it's fine to speak up, who actually decides things. You're expected to know them, and no one tells you what they are.

You learn them by watching. The students who seem to fit in quickly usually aren't more social. They're paying attention.

<Mark>The rules that matter most in a new place are the ones nobody says out loud. You learn them by watching, and the cost of not watching is looking like you don't get it.</Mark>

### Watch before you speak

Walking into a new room and immediately asserting yourself is how you misread it. Give the first stretch, the first weeks in a club, the first months in a job, to observing how the place actually works.

Notice who people listen to, how formal things are, how disagreement gets handled, what's said in the open versus one-on-one. Then you speak with the room instead of against it. This is not about staying quiet forever. It is about learning the room before you test it.

### Disagree in private, not in front of the group

You will sometimes think a plan is wrong, and you might be right. How you raise it decides whether it lands.

In most Indian workplaces and teams, pushing back on someone in front of the whole group reads as undermining them, not as helping. The same point made one-on-one gets heard. Save the real disagreement for a private conversation. In the open, you get the person defensive. In private, you get them thinking.

### Seniors are the best source on campus, and nobody asks

The people one and two years ahead of you already survived the exact things you're worried about: the professor to take, the company that visited, the subject that wrecked everyone, what they'd redo. It's the most relevant information you can get, and most students never ask for any of it.

Ask, and ask specifically. "Any advice?" gets a shrug. "How did you prepare for that company's online assessment, and what surprised you?" gets a real answer. A specific question is easy to answer and shows you did some thinking first.

### Being seen busy isn't being useful

There's a temptation, in college and in your first job, to perform effort: the late nights, the constant visible grind, being the last one working. It gets read as commitment for a while.

It doesn't hold up. What builds a reputation is finished, clear work that people can rely on, not how visibly tired you look getting there. Optimize for the thing you can point to at the end, not for looking busy in the middle.

### Your network is the people in front of you now

The network you imagine building "later" is mostly the people you're already standing next to: your project group, the person beside you at a hackathon, the senior who answered your question, the batchmate you explained a concept to.

Keep track of them. After a hackathon or a talk or a good thread, note who the person was and what they were into. It feels unnecessary in the moment. Two years later, when you need a referral or an introduction, it's the difference between a warm message and a cold one.
