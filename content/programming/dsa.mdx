DSA is one of those things where the advice is everywhere and almost all of it is too much or too little. Too much: "solve a thousand problems." Too little: "just understand the concepts."

Here is a more grounded version.

### What DSA Actually Is

Data structures are ways of organizing data so that operations on it are efficient. An array lets you access any element in constant time. A linked list makes insertion at the head cheap. A hash map trades memory for lookup speed.

Algorithms are step-by-step procedures for solving problems. Sorting, searching, pathfinding, optimization. Most of what you encounter in placement interviews comes from a set of patterns that repeat across problems. <Mark>You don't need to know a thousand problems. You need to know fifty patterns well enough to recognize which one applies.</Mark>

The two things are connected: understanding what data structure to use for a problem often tells you what algorithm is appropriate.

### The Placement Prep Reality

Indian placement prep through DSA has a specific shape. The companies you're targeting determine how deep you need to go.

| Target | What You Need |
|--------|--------------|
| Service companies | Basic data structures, simple sorting and searching, aptitude |
| Mid-tier product companies | Medium-level problems, core patterns, standard CS subjects |
| Top product companies | Medium to hard problems, system design basics, strong fundamentals |
| Most selective companies | Hard problems consistently, system design, behavioral interviews |

Most students should aim for the second or third tier and adjust from there based on where they're getting calls.

### Where to Start

If you haven't touched DSA at all, start with arrays and strings. They're in almost every interview and mastering them gives you the foundation everything else builds on.

The recommended progression:

1. Arrays and strings
2. Two pointers and sliding window
3. Hashing (HashMap, HashSet)
4. Linked lists
5. Stacks and queues
6. Binary search (on arrays, then on answers)
7. Trees (traversals, BST, problems)
8. Heaps and priority queues
9. Graphs (BFS, DFS, shortest paths)
10. Dynamic programming

Don't treat this as a strict order where you can't move until the previous section is perfect. Work through it and revisit earlier topics as new ones make you see them differently.

Search for structured topic lists and problem sheets for Indian placement prep. Several comprehensive ones exist. Cross-reference a few and pick one to work through consistently.

<Callout type="info">
Practice on the platforms that placement tests actually use. Most Indian companies use specific online assessment platforms. Familiarity with the interface and time pressure matters.
</Callout>

### How to Actually Practice

The mistake most people make: they read the solution when they're stuck after five minutes.

Sit with a problem for thirty to forty-five minutes before looking at a hint. If you haven't solved it by then, read the hint only, not the solution. Try again. If you still can't get it, read the approach in plain language, close the solution, and write the code yourself. Never copy a solution and call it done.

After solving a problem, explain it out loud as if you're explaining it to someone. If you can't explain it, you didn't understand it. You memorized it. There's a difference and interviewers notice.

<Mark>Track what you get wrong. A list of ten problems you keep failing is more useful than a count of two hundred problems you've done.</Mark>

Keep a problem journal. For each problem you struggle with, write: what pattern did I miss, why did my first approach fail, what would I do differently next time.

### Competitive Programming vs Placement Prep

These are different activities. They overlap, but the overlap is smaller than people assume.

Competitive programming involves time-limited contests with problems that often require advanced mathematical insight, advanced data structures, and a level of speed that most industry problems don't require.

Placement prep involves a narrower set of patterns that come up repeatedly in interviews, asked at a slower pace, with more focus on explaining your thinking.

<Callout type="warn">
Competitive programming is not required for placements. It helps, especially for the most selective companies. But students who spend all of second and third year on contest platforms and neglect development skills, projects, and communication often interview worse than students with solid medium-level practice and real projects to talk about.
</Callout>

If you enjoy CP and are good at it, do it. It will make placement prep easier. If you don't enjoy it and are doing it because someone said you have to, you're probably spending time you could use better.
