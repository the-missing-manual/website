---
title: Choosing a Language
description: How to think about the language question without someone else making the decision for you.
---

This guide does not recommend a programming language. That is a deliberate choice.

The language question has no universal answer. The right one depends on what you want to build, what the roles you're targeting use, what your course requires, and what you're willing to spend time learning. Someone else's recommendation shortcuts a reasoning process you should go through yourself.

### Start With What You Want to Build

Different domains have different dominant tools. Not because other languages can't do those things, but because ecosystems, tooling, and hiring expectations have coalesced around certain choices in each space.

Research the domain you're interested in. Look at job listings. Look at what tools are used in open source projects in that space. Look at developer surveys from the past year or two. Patterns will emerge quickly.

If you have no domain preference yet, pick the problem you want to solve and let that lead you to the domain.

### Questions to Answer Before Deciding

- What do people use in the area I want to work in?
- What does my course expect me to know?
- What are companies I'm interested in actually hiring for?
- Is there a large enough community that I can get help when I'm stuck?
- Are there enough learning resources available?

Useful searches: `[domain] programming language [year]`, `[domain] developer survey`, `most used languages for [domain]`, `[domain] job listings`.

Read it, verify its sources, and cross-reference with your own research. No single document should be the end of that inquiry.

### The Mistake to Avoid

Spending months debating language choice instead of building something. Pick one based on your research, build something with it, and re-evaluate after you've finished a real project. You'll know far more at that point than you do now.

<Mark>A finished project in any language teaches you more than six months of reading about the perfect language choice.</Mark>
