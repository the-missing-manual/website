DSA gets you through the filter. Projects get you the offer.

In most technical interviews, the conversation at some point turns to "walk me through your most interesting project." Everything you've built, deployed, and broken in real conditions is material for that conversation.

<Mark>A real project is one that does something, not one that demonstrates that you learned something.</Mark> The difference is visible immediately. "I built a practice app to learn X" reads as practice. "I built a tool that I use every week to track X" reads as a builder.

### Picking a Domain

Before picking tools, pick the type of thing you want to build. The domain shapes what you learn, what you build, and what roles you can apply for.

Some broad directions:

- **Frontend:** What users see and interact with. Interfaces, browser behavior, design systems.
- **Backend:** The logic behind the interface. APIs, databases, authentication, jobs that run in the background.
- **Full Stack:** Both sides. Takes longer to reach real depth, but lets you ship complete products.
- **Mobile:** Apps that run on phones. Two major platforms, each with its own ecosystem.
- **Systems:** Compilers, operating systems, networking tools, infrastructure. Requires a stronger foundational base.
- **Data:** Working with large datasets, pipelines, analysis. Requires a solid math foundation.

Pick one and go deep. The mistake most students make is touching all of them and going deep on none.

Research what people in each domain actually do day to day. Job listings, engineering blogs, communities in that space. Talk to people who work in roles you're interested in.

### Choosing Your Tools

Once you know the domain, research what the industry uses in that space. This changes over time and varies by company size, region, and role. Don't take a recommendation from a single source. Cross-reference across:

- Job listings for roles you want (what do they list as requirements?)
- Recent developer surveys (search for annual developer surveys from major platforms)
- Communities in that domain

Search for: `[domain] developer tools [year]`, `[domain] developer roadmap`, `what [domain] developers use`.

<Callout type="info">
Different employers use different tools. The goal isn't to learn one correct stack. The goal is to understand a domain well enough that switching tools is a matter of weeks, not months.
</Callout>

### What to Build

The best project ideas come from your own annoyances. What do you do every day that's slower than it should be? What do you wish existed?

If nothing comes to mind, start with something small in the domain you chose. A working thing that does one thing well is better than an ambitious thing that doesn't run. Finishing a small project teaches you more than reading about a larger one.

As the project grows, you'll run into real problems. Why is this slow? How do I store this data? How do other people solve this? Those problems teach you to think like an engineer.

Deployment matters. A project with a live URL or a working binary is worth more than one that only runs on your laptop. Search for free hosting options for the type of project you're building.

### Open Source

Contributing to open source gives you something specific: you read other people's code, which is most of what professional programming actually is. You practice collaboration through version control. You end up with something on your public profile that shows you can work in an existing codebase, not just your own.

How to start:

1. Find a project you actually use. Something you care about.
2. Read the contributing guide. Most projects have one.
3. Look for issues labeled `good first issue` or `help wanted`.
4. Start small. Documentation, a failing test, a reproducible bug report. Your first contribution doesn't need to be impressive.

Search for `good first issues [domain]` to find starting points. There are also aggregator sites that collect these issues across projects. Student open source programs run annually with applications opening months in advance. Search for them in your area of interest.

<Callout type="warn">
Don't contribute to open source just to have it on your resume. Interviewers can tell the difference between someone who found a bug they actually cared about and someone who fixed a typo to have a checkmark on their profile.
</Callout>

### What Makes a Project Resume-Worthy

Not every project belongs on a resume. What makes the difference:

**It solves a real problem.** Even a small one. "I built this because I was annoyed by X" is a story. "I built this to practice X" is not.

**It's deployed and accessible.** A live URL, a working download, something someone can actually use.

**You can explain the decisions.** Why did you structure the data that way? Why did you choose that approach over the obvious one? If you can't answer these, you don't understand your own project well enough.

**It has numbers where possible.** Users, requests served, time saved, data processed. Even small numbers. "Around 50 people from my college use it" is meaningful.

<Mark>One project you can talk about deeply beats five projects you can only describe at the surface level.</Mark>
