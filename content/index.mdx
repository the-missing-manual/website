If you found this, you're in one of a few places.

You're trying to figure out which college to join and every source has given you a different answer. Or you're already in a computing program and the gap between what you're studying and what the industry wants feels wider than it should. Or someone rattled off "learn DSA, build projects, get internships, maintain CGPA, contribute to open source" in one breath and left before explaining any of it.

This exists because finding reliable information is harder than it should be. The same questions come up in the same servers and comment sections every week. The answers here are collected from students who went through it, made mistakes, and knew what they'd do differently.

<Callout type="warn" title="Before you go any further">
This is not an authoritative guide. Nothing here comes from industry research, official bodies, or anyone with credentials worth listing.

It's built from personal experiences: students who went through the process, made mistakes, and wrote down what they'd do differently. <Mark>Some of it will be wrong. Some of it will be outdated by the time you read it.</Mark> Some of it reflects what worked in one context and won't apply to yours.

Read it as exactly what it is: perspectives from people who were in the same position as you, not that long ago. Use your own judgment. Cross-check anything that matters.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="How this guide works">
This guide does not recommend specific tools, languages, platforms, courses, or colleges. It does not favor any institution, bootcamp, YouTube channel, or product. Nobody paid for placement here. Nothing gets listed because of a partnership or affiliation.

It applies to anyone in a computing-related program, whether that's a two-year diploma, a three-year degree, a four-year engineering program, or anything else. The year-based sections are a rough framework, not a rigid calendar.

The goal is to give you the right questions to ask and the right things to look for, so you can find answers that fit your situation. Nobody else can make those decisions for you and this guide doesn't try to.
</Callout>

### How to Use This

Don't read this from start to finish. Use it like documentation: go to what you need, skip what you don't, come back when it's relevant.

- Picking a college? Start with **College Admissions**.
- Starting out and not sure where to begin? Go to **Learning to Program**.
- Already enrolled and figuring out what to do year by year? Go to **The College Journey**.
- Looking for tools and how to find them? Check **Resources**.
- Struggling with the grind of placement season? Read **Wellbeing**.

The goal isn't to teach every topic. It's to point you toward the right place to look, and to keep you from spending months going in the wrong direction because someone gave you half an answer.
