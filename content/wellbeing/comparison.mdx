---
title: Comparison and Imposter Syndrome
description: Why the feed is a highlight reel, why you feel like a fraud, and how to measure against the only person who counts.
---

Placement season turns your batchmates into a scoreboard. LinkedIn fills with offer announcements. The group chat becomes a running tally of who got what. Everyone looks further ahead than you, and it eats your focus at the exact moment you need it.

The scoreboard is mostly fake, and it still does real damage. Naming how it works takes some of its power away.

<Mark>You're comparing your behind-the-scenes to everyone else's highlight reel. Nobody posts the thirty rejections behind the one "excited to announce."</Mark>

### The feed is a highlight reel

People post offers, not the rejections that came first. They post the internship, not the four months of no replies. What you scroll past is a filtered stream of everyone's best moments, stacked together until it looks like the whole world is winning except you.

This is survivorship bias, and it's built into the platform. The student who got placed on day one posts about it. The one still interviewing in month six stays quiet. You see the first and forget the second exists, even though the second is far more common.

### Compare against yourself last month

The only honest comparison is you against an earlier version of you. Could you solve this class of problem last month. Did that concept make sense in September. That baseline is real, and it's the one you can actually move.

Ranking yourself against the person next to you is noise. They started somewhere different, want something different, and are hiding most of their struggle the same way you're hiding yours. See the same idea applied to learning in [How to Learn](/small-things/learning).

### Imposter syndrome is nearly universal

The feeling that you don't really belong, that you got lucky, that any moment someone will find out you're not as capable as they think, is close to universal in this field. It shows up hardest right after something goes well: after you get into a good college, after you get placed, after you land the internship.

Strong engineers feel it too. People years into good careers feel it. It is not evidence that you're a fraud. It's evidence that you're doing something hard enough to stretch you, around people who are also stretching. Naming it as imposter syndrome, out loud, is most of the fix.

### What actually helps

- **Mute the feed during intense stretches.** You don't need a live scoreboard of other people's offers while you're mid-grind. Turn it off for a few weeks.
- **Talk about the struggle, not just the wins.** The comparison spiral breaks the moment you find out the person you envy is also anxious and also getting rejected. Honest conversations do that.
- **Measure against your own targets.** Decide what you're aiming for and track progress toward that, not toward a moving average of your batch.

<Callout type="info">
Comparison and burnout feed each other. Watching everyone else while running yourself into the ground is a fast way to both. See [Burnout](/wellbeing/burnout).
</Callout>
