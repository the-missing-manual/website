title: Burnout
description: The difference between tired and burnt out, the warning signs, and why rest is part of the work, not a break from it.

Tiredness lifts after a weekend. Burnout doesn't. Months of running at full intensity, with no recovery scheduled in, produces a specific kind of depletion that sleep alone stops fixing. Students hit it during long placement seasons, and also during ordinary semesters where the pace never lets up.

<Mark>Burnout is not weakness or laziness. It's what happens when the intensity never drops and recovery never gets scheduled. The fix is a change of pace, not trying harder.</Mark>

### Tired is not the same as burnt out

Tiredness is physical. You feel it, you rest, it goes. Burnout is deeper and has a different shape:

- **Exhaustion** that a good night's sleep or a weekend doesn't touch.
- **Cynicism** about the whole thing. The work you cared about starts to feel pointless.
- **Dropping output** even as you spend more hours, because the hours have stopped producing much.

If a full weekend of rest leaves you still dreading Monday's laptop, that's not tiredness anymore.

### The warning signs

Burnout builds slowly, so it's easy to miss until you're deep in it. Common early signs:

- Dread at the thought of opening your editor or your notes.
- Small tasks feeling disproportionately heavy.
- No amount of rest feeling restful.
- Losing track of why you're doing any of it.
- Snapping at people, or going numb.

Catching these early is the difference between adjusting your pace and losing a month.

### The grind is a season, not a sprint

Running at maximum in the first month leaves nothing for the sixth. Placement season and hard semesters both run long, and the students who stay functional through them are the ones who paced deliberately, not the ones who went hardest early. This is the same point the [Placement Season](/wellbeing/placement-season) page makes about recovery, and it holds year-round.

### Getting out of it

- **Rest that's actually rest.** Three hours of scrolling is not recovery. A walk, real sleep, time with people, something that resets you.
- **Drop the intensity on purpose.** Stepping back for a few days recovers more usable output than grinding through on empty. Counterintuitive, and true.
- **Set boundaries.** You can't be available to every group, every deadline, every opportunity at once. Saying no to some of it is how you keep doing any of it well.

<Callout type="warn">
If the exhaustion and low mood persist for weeks and rest isn't touching them, treat it as more than burnout. Talk to someone you trust, or a professional. Most colleges have counsellors, and using them is not a failure. Struggling quietly for months is the more expensive path.
</Callout>
