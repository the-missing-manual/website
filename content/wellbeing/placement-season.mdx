Nobody talks about this part. The advice is always about algorithms and resume formatting. Nobody mentions that a seven-round interview day, plus three companies in the same week, plus not knowing if you have a job yet, is genuinely exhausting in a physical way.

<Mark>A bad night of sleep before a technical round affects your problem-solving more than one extra day of preparation would have helped.</Mark> This is not motivational content. It's physiology.

### What Interview Season Actually Looks Like

Placement season typically runs for several months. In that period you might:

- Give aptitude tests every week for two or three months
- Have back-to-back technical rounds on the same day with different companies
- Stay up late preparing for a company, only to have them cancel
- Sit in holding rooms for hours waiting for your turn
- Handle the emotional load of rejections while continuing to apply

This is not a sprint. It's a multi-month process that runs alongside your coursework. Students who treat it as a sprint burn out early. Students who treat it as a season manage their energy and stay functional through the end.

### Sleep

The first thing to protect, and the first thing most students sacrifice.

Coding at 2 AM the night before a round feels like preparation. It usually isn't. Working memory degrades measurably after seventeen to nineteen hours of being awake. A problem that takes you twenty minutes well-rested can take forty-five minutes sleep-deprived.

Set a hard stop time the night before any interview or test. That stop time should be at least eight hours before you need to be sharp. What you don't finish after that time would not have saved you anyway.

If you haven't fixed a concept by the night before, you won't fix it in the two hours before you sleep. Spend those hours reviewing what you already know.

<Callout type="warn">
Pulling an all-nighter before a technical round is almost always counterproductive. You'll be slower, you'll miss things you'd normally catch, and you'll second-guess correct answers you'd normally be confident about. Sleep is preparation.
</Callout>

### Movement

Sitting for long stretches, high ambient anxiety, erratic eating, and irregular sleep push your nervous system toward a low-grade chronic stress state. Consistent physical movement is the most effective non-pharmaceutical intervention for that state.

You don't need a gym. You need consistent movement.

**A morning walk.** Twenty to thirty minutes before you start studying. This isn't fitness advice, it's a cognitive tool. Morning light and movement bring stress hormones down from overnight levels. Students who do this tend to have better focus in the first two hours of the day.

**Resistance movement two to three times a week.** Basic bodyweight movement has a durable effect on mood and stress resilience. You don't need equipment. You need consistency.

**Light movement before long interview days.** Ten to fifteen minutes of movement before you sit in a room for hours. It drops cortisol enough to matter in hour five of a long day.

What to avoid: training hard the day before a critical round. Moderate movement helps. Soreness from overexertion doesn't.

### Eating During Interview Days

Long interview days start early and end late. If you don't eat, cognitive performance drops in predictable and preventable ways.

Eat breakfast before you go. Something with protein and slow carbs. Not just coffee.

Carry something. A banana, some nuts, anything dry. You don't know when the next break will be. Interview schedules rarely run on time.

Avoid a heavy meal before a technical round if you can. A large meal in the middle of the day slows you down. Lighter works better when you need to think.

Stay hydrated. Mild dehydration produces fatigue and concentration issues that are difficult to distinguish from anxiety, but go away when you drink water.

### Managing Anxiety

Some anxiety before an interview is functional. It sharpens attention. Too much crosses into a state where working memory degrades and you blank on things you know.

What actually helps:

**The physiological sigh.** A double inhale through the nose, then a long slow exhale through the mouth. One or two of these activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the acute anxiety response. It works and it takes thirty seconds.

**Concrete preparation lists.** Anxiety is highest when you feel out of control. A done list of what you've covered, what you've reviewed, and what logistics you've sorted reduces the ambient uncertainty that feeds anxiety.

**Naming what you don't know.** It sounds counterintuitive, but naming specific gaps is less stressful than vague worry about everything. "I'm weak on graph problems" is a manageable fact. Vague worry is worse.

<Callout type="info">
If you blank on a problem during an interview, say so directly. "I'm working through this, let me think out loud." Interviewers evaluate your reasoning process as much as the solution. A visible thinking process is better than silence followed by a wrong answer.
</Callout>

### Recovery Between Rounds

You won't hear from a company for two weeks, then three things happen in the same week. That's how it goes.

Between intensive periods, actually rest. Not scrolling for three hours. Rest. A walk, a conversation with someone not in placement season, a meal you eat slowly. Whatever resets you.

Don't use every quiet week to run at maximum intensity on preparation. You'll exhaust yourself before the busy weeks. Use quiet weeks to maintain, not to sprint.

<Mark>The students who stay functional through all of placement season are the ones who figured out their recovery routine, not the ones who worked the hardest in the first month.</Mark>
