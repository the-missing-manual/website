---
title: How to Ask for Help
description: How to ask technical questions in a way that gets real answers.
---

The way you ask a technical question determines the quality of the answer you get. A question missing key details gets one of two responses: no response, or a response asking for the details you left out.

Getting this right is a skill. It takes practice. The payoff is that people with more knowledge are willing to help you.

### What a Good Question Looks Like

Before posting a question anywhere, have answers to these:

**What are you trying to do?** Not what the code says you're doing. What the actual goal is.

**What have you tried?** List the things you've already attempted. This shows you've put in effort and helps people skip advice that won't work.

**What happened?** Exact output, exact error messages, exact behavior. Copy and paste, don't paraphrase. A paraphrased error message misses information that matters.

**What did you expect to happen?** The difference between what happened and what you expected is the gap where the answer lives.

**Minimal reproducible example.** The most important one. If you can reduce your problem to the smallest possible piece of code that still shows the problem, do it. This forces you to understand the problem better and makes it much easier for others to help. Sometimes you solve it yourself in the process.

### Where to Read More

StackOverflow has a guide on asking good questions. It is worth reading once: `stackoverflow.com/help/how-to-ask`

Eric Raymond and Rick Moen wrote a detailed document called "How to Ask Questions the Smart Way." It is widely referenced in technical communities. Search for it. The section on how to write a useful question is the most relevant part. Some of the tone is dated but the principles are sound.

LiveOverflow made a video about how not to ask technical questions. Search for it on YouTube by name. Watching it once is faster than reading the same points in text.

<Callout type="info">
These are starting points. The best way to get better at asking questions is to ask them, notice which ones get useful responses, and figure out why.
</Callout>

### Community Norms

Different communities have different cultures. Before posting in any community, read recent posts to understand the tone and expectations. Most communities have pinned rules or FAQ threads.

In most technical communities:

- Search before asking. If the question has been answered before, find that answer first.
- One question per post. Multiple questions in one post split the attention of people trying to help.
- Respond to follow-up questions promptly.
- Mark the solution when you find it. Other people with the same problem will search later and find your thread.

When giving feedback on someone else's question or code, be constructive. "This can be done differently" with a concrete alternative is useful. "This is bad" or "this approach sucks" is not. Simply saying something is wrong without providing a better solution and reasoning behind it doesn't help anyone.

### When You Don't Get a Response

Sometimes a question gets no response. Before reposting:

- Did you follow the community's question format?
- Is your example actually minimal?
- Did you include the exact error and exact context?
- Are you in the right community for that topic?

Revise the question, not the community. The problem is usually in how the question was framed.
