The number a company announces and the number that reaches your bank are not the same, and nobody slows down to explain the gap before you sign. This page is about getting real figures and reading an offer for what it actually pays.

This is the one topic where numbers matter more than advice, so it leans on data sources, named here for the same reason as the [Learning Resources](/resources/learning): they're credible and not selling you anything.

<Mark>Cost to Company (CTC) is what the company spends on you, not what you take home. Your in-hand is usually 60 to 75 percent of the CTC, and the headline is built to look larger than that.</Mark>

### Why the headline is bigger than your salary

CTC bundles in money you never see as monthly salary:

- **Employer provident fund.** The company's contribution to your Employee Provident Fund (EPF) counts in CTC but goes to your PF account, not your bank.
- **Gratuity accrual.** Around 4.81 percent of basic salary is counted every year, but you only receive it after five continuous years.
- **Variable pay and joining bonuses.** Performance pay that depends on ratings, and one-time bonuses that inflate year one and vanish after.
- **Stock, where it exists.** Often vesting over years, sometimes worth less than the number on paper.

After you strip those out, income tax and professional tax come off what's left. The [Placements & ROI](/choice/placements) page breaks down CTC inflation in full. This page is about where to look up the real numbers.

### Where to find real numbers

All of these are self-reported, so treat them as ranges, not guarantees. Cross-check across a few before you believe any single figure.

| Source | What it's good for |
|--------|--------------------|
| [levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) | Detailed compensation for product and multinational companies, broken into base, stock, and bonus. Strongest for larger firms. |
| [AmbitionBox](https://www.ambitionbox.com/salaries) | The widest Indian salary coverage, including service companies and smaller firms. Also has a CTC-to-in-hand calculator. |
| [Glassdoor](https://www.glassdoor.co.in/Salaries/index.htm) | Salary ranges plus interview experiences and reviews. Cross-reference, don't trust one review. |

### Turn a CTC into a take-home figure

Before you compare two offers, convert both to monthly in-hand. A CTC-to-in-hand calculator does this, accounting for PF, professional tax, and income tax. Reputable tax platforms host free ones. Search for a "CTC to in-hand salary calculator" and use a well-known one, then check its assumptions match your situation.

### Read the offer, not the number

When an offer arrives, get the breakdown in writing before you decide:

- **Fixed versus variable.** What is guaranteed monthly, and what depends on ratings or company performance.
- **The one-time components.** Joining bonus and relocation inflate the first year. What does year two look like without them.
- **The bond or service agreement.** Some companies require you to stay a set period or pay a penalty. Know the amount and the term before signing.
- **Stock terms.** Vesting schedule, and whether the valuation is real or notional.

<Callout type="info">
Two offers with the same CTC can pay very different amounts in hand. A service company at 6 LPA fixed can out-pay a product company quoting 8 LPA that's half variable and stock. Compare in-hand, not headlines. For deciding between offers and reading placement data, see [Placements & ROI](/choice/placements) and [After College](/college/after-college).
</Callout>
