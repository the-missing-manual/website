title: Learning Resources
description: A short list of super-credible, mostly-free resources for the parts of the field that outlast any language, from concepts to system design.
---

The rest of this guide names no tools, no channels, no courses. This page is the exception, and it's a narrow one.

Everything below teaches concepts, principles, or system design, the parts of the field that stay true no matter which language or framework you end up in. You won't find a "learn HTML and CSS" or "learn this framework" course here. Those change fast, depend entirely on what you're building, and this guide stays out of picking your stack. For a tutorial on a specific tool, see [Searching Effectively](/resources/google-search) and [Awesome Lists](/resources/awesome-lists).

Everything here is widely respected, mostly free, and unaffiliated with this guide. Nobody paid to be listed. These are starting points, not a syllabus and not an order to follow. A resource you finish beats ten you bookmark.

<Mark>The list is short on purpose. The problem was never a shortage of good resources. It's that the good ones get buried under a hundred paid ones fighting for your attention.</Mark>

<Callout type="warn">
A resource is only useful if you use it. Watching a full course at 2x while typing nothing is entertainment, not learning. Build alongside anything you watch or read.
</Callout>

### CS foundations

The theory a degree is supposed to cover, and often skims. All free.

| Resource | What it's for |
|----------|---------------|
| [Teach Yourself CS](https://teachyourselfcs.com/) | A short, opinionated list of the nine subjects that matter and the one best book or course for each. Start here if you want a map. |
| [OSSU Computer Science](https://github.com/ossu/computer-science) | A full self-taught CS education built from free university courses, in order. The most complete free curriculum there is. |
| [CS50](https://cs50.harvard.edu/x/) | Harvard's intro to computer science. Free to audit, famously well-produced, and a strong first course if you're starting cold. |
| [MIT Missing Semester](https://missing.csail.mit.edu/) | The tools no class teaches: the shell, editors, debugging, the command line. Short, and it fills a real gap. |

### Algorithms and interview prep

Algorithmic thinking is a principle, not a stack, so it belongs here. See [DSA](/programming/dsa) for how to approach it without burning out.

| Resource | What it's for |
|----------|---------------|
| [NeetCode](https://neetcode.io/) | A curated set of interview problems grouped by pattern, with clear video solutions. The pattern grouping is the value. |
| [Tech Interview Handbook](https://www.techinterviewhandbook.org/) | A free, no-nonsense guide to the whole interview process, plus a curated question list. |
| [takeUforward / Striver](https://takeuforward.org/) | Structured DSA sheets and video explanations aimed at Indian placement prep. Strong free content, optional paid tier. |
| [CP-Algorithms](https://cp-algorithms.com/) | Clear reference explanations and implementations for algorithms, from standard to advanced. |
| [VisuAlgo](https://visualgo.net/) | Animated visualizations of data structures and algorithms. Good when a concept won't click from text. |
| [Abdul Bari (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/@abdul_bari) | Deep, patient explanations of algorithms and their theory. The reference many students fall back to. |

### System design and backend

Mostly relevant later, from third year on, once fundamentals are in place.

| Resource | What it's for |
|----------|---------------|
| [System Design Primer](https://github.com/donnemartin/system-design-primer) | The standard free starting point for system design interviews. Broad and well-organized. |
| [Arpit Bhayani (Asli Engineering)](https://www.youtube.com/@AsliEngineering) | Ex-Google and Razorpay engineer who goes into how databases and distributed systems actually work, not just interview templates. Free YouTube, optional paid masterclass. |
| [Gaurav Sen (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/@gkcs) | Approachable walkthroughs of system design problems. A common first exposure to the topic. |
| [Hussein Nasser (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/@hnasr) | Backend engineering, databases, and networking explained from first principles. |
| [Designing Data-Intensive Applications](https://dataintensive.net/) | The book on how real data systems work. Dense, worth it once you have some backend experience. |

### Version control

A universal engineering practice, not a stack. See [Version Control](/programming/git) for what to learn and the pitfalls.

| Resource | What it's for |
|----------|---------------|
| [Pro Git book](https://git-scm.com/book/en/v2) | The free, official git book. Chapters 1 to 5 cover most of what you'll use. |
| [Learn Git Branching](https://learngitbranching.js.org/) | An interactive game for understanding branching and merging visually. The fastest way to stop being scared of git. |
| [MIT Missing Semester: Git](https://missing.csail.mit.edu/2020/version-control/) | Git explained through its underlying data model, which makes the commands stop feeling random. |
| [Dangit, Git!?!](https://dangitgit.com/) | Plain-English fixes for the "I broke git, now what" moments. Bookmark it before you need it. |

### Going deeper

Free books and courses for understanding a layer below what you use. Not urgent, and worth knowing they exist.

| Resource | What it's for |
|----------|---------------|
| [OSTEP](https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~remzi/OSTEP/) | Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces. A free, readable operating systems textbook. |
| [Crafting Interpreters](https://craftinginterpreters.com/) | Build a real programming language from scratch. Free online, and it demystifies a lot. |
| [Nand2Tetris](https://www.nand2tetris.org/) | Build a computer from logic gates up to a working program. Changes how you see the whole stack. |
| [3Blue1Brown (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/@3blue1brown) | The clearest visual math on the internet. Linear algebra, calculus, and the ideas behind neural networks. |
| [Andrej Karpathy (YouTube)](https://www.youtube.com/@AndrejKarpathy) | Neural Networks: Zero to Hero. Building modern machine learning from scratch, line by line. |
